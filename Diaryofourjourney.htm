October 29, 2000

The drive through Wyoming was pretty nice a first, beautiful country with mountains, ranches, cows, real cowboys, and other different animals.  But after a while there were no towns, only one or two roads and not much else.  Montana was a lot of the same.  Finally after several days of driving we arrived at the western entrance to Yellowstone National Park.  Our first drive into Yellowstone proved to be a little difficult.  On our way into the park we ran into a heard of buffalo, literally.  The Buffalo were all over the road, there must have been about 30 of them, small ones, little ones, and big huge ones.  They came right up next to the car, fortunately none of them decided to come into the car.  We also saw geysers of all different types, hot springs with beautiful clear blue and yellow colors, and when we were walking around the one hot spring area a heard of elk decided to walk around with us.  One even walked right behind Giustina.  We saw Old Faithful, although it was kind of a let down, just a big spray of water in the air.  The other geysers were much prettier with a lot more color than Old Faithful.  That evening we had a really good meal at a local restaurant, steaks really are better in Wyoming and Montana.

 

After leaving Yellowstone we drove through Idaho on our way to Seattle, WA about 900 miles away.  We first stopped in Twin Falls, ID and saw their local waterfalls.  They weren’t very spectacular since it was the end of fall and not much water was coming over them, but I imagine in the spring time they would look pretty amazing.  After eating so much read meat in Wyoming and Montana we had a good sea food dinner at a restaurant called Rock Creek. After we left Twin Falls we drove through the Craters of the Moon National Monument which is the site of several volcanic explosions. 

 

We then drove up the western side of Idaho.  We stopped at a winery on the way and got the RV stuck in a parking lot trying to turn around.  When we realized we couldn’t get the RV to move a very nice person asked if we needed help.  Of course we said yes because we really had no other choice and before we knew it he went in the back and brought out a John Deere tractor and chain to help pull the RV out.  It was a pretty small tractor in comparison with the RV so we didn’t know if it would work.  Before we had a chance to try it though another man came out with a HUGE backhoe/tractor that was at least as large as the RV.  We hooked the chain up to this huge mammoth beast and in two seconds we were unstuck.  We tried to go out and thank both men but before we could they were gone, just like that.  It was an amazingly nice thing that they did for us and really helped us out of a jam.  We continued on to a very nice winery, and drank and bought some really good wine.  We continued our drive and stopped at a gas station.  As I was filling the tank a random person noticed that our spare tire on the RV was falling off, probably because of the last incident.  Before I could get the toolbox out and try and get it back on the guy was under the back of the RV putting it back into place.  Again before I could offer them something for their help they took off.  Again an amazing gesture of kindness, you would never find this kind of help in downtown Boston.  We continued on our drive on a two way one lane road that half way through turned into a torn up road that was just gravel and huge potholes.  When we finally found some more pavement and took a look at the white car we were towing it was pitch black.  We finally found an RV park and stopped for the night.

October 22, 2000

After spending some time on the Mississippi it was off to the twin cities.  We picked a campground on the edge of the city in a town called Prior Lake.  From reading the description it seemed like it would be a nice place to stay including a heated pool, sauna, and whirlpool.  When we arrived at the campground we realized we picked a campground that was part of a local casino.  And instead of the campground being a nicely wooded area outside the twin cities, it was actually a cement paved glorified parking lot.  All of the spaces in the “park” were paved and there were street lights near every spot.  It looked like the campground was just a section of the parking lot for the casino.  In fact the first evening as we went to walk to the casino we heard some music being broadcast over loud speakers in the parking lot.  By 11 PM when we turned in for the night the music was still playing and by 3 AM as I rolled over in bed the music was still playing.  It was like Chinese water torture with music.  We awoke the next day only slightly rested given our crummy nights sleep and planned our attack on the Mall of America.  For those of you who haven’t been to The Mall of America I will try and describe it.  The mall is about four to five times as large as any other mall and encompasses a total of 78 acres.  In fact it contains over 520 stores, just about every store you could possibly think of, and if that was not enough in the middle of the mall inside it there is an amusement park.  The park includes a roller coaster, log flume ride, Ferris wheel, and merry go round.  All in all outside of the overall size of the mall it was nothing special, just a REALLY HUGE mall.  I can’t even image what it must be like the day after Thanksgiving.  The next day after we awoke from our Chinese water torture music serenade, we decided to check out of the RV campground, leave the RV in the casino lot, and head into downtown Minneapolis.  We drove into town and had breakfast at a place recommended by the book Eat Your Way Across the USA.  The place was called Al’s Breakfast and consisted of a room that was about 12 feet wide by 30 feet long.  It had a counter the entire length of the room with a single row of seats.  You literally had to squeeze past the stools looking for a seat at the counter.  We found a seat, took a look at the menu and ordered breakfast.  Both of our breakfasts were delicious.  You could smell the grease and fat in the air.  It was one of the best breakfast joints we have ever been too.  After breakfast we spent some time walking around the downtown Minneapolis sky way.  We then drove back to the RV and on to our next destination South Dakota.

 

On our way to South Dakota we planned to stop by one of the legendary icons of roadside America, The Largest Ball of Twine.  It took us quite a while to get to the Ball of Twine and by the time we arrived we were very anxious to see it.  Unfortunately it wasn’t a very memorable experience.  The town with the Ball of Twine has at most 200 people and the Ball of Twine is on a side road in an outdoor gazebo all by itself.  They even enclosed it in glass so you can’t even touch it.  Even the gift shop, if you can call it that, was closed.  It was kind of a let down and on top of it all it started to rain.  Well after driving over four hours to see a bunch of string enclosed in glass we still had quite a few hours left till our next destination, so we decided to just drive for a couple more hours and park for the night.  We couldn’t find a campground near by so we ended up staying in a rest stop on the side of the road.  If you have ever stayed in a road side rest stop you no that they are not exactly quiet, in fact the tractor trailers are quite loud.  The next morning as we awoke from our crummy half nights sleep we knew we had quite a few miles left to go on our way to Mount Rushmore.  We started our drive through more of South Dakota and stopped by another roadside icon, The Corn Palace.  The Corn Palace, located in Mitchell, SD, is basically a building covered in corn.  It’s a pretty large building on top of it, about the size of a small stadium.  It’s also the only thing in town outside of your standard tourist trap stores.  The residents even change the corn on the building once a year, what a country…  After the Corn Palace we continued our drive, and drove, and drove, and then when we though we were somewhere we drove some more.  By the time we realized that we still had four hours to Mount Rushmore we were in the middle of no where, it was dark, and we were exhausted.  We got off the road we were on in a local town and tried to drive around the town looking for a campground or rest area.  Unfortunately none were found, in fact we had a hard time even finding a paved road in the middle of town.  When we stopped to look at the map to see if there was anything else in the area, we had the pleasure of meeting a local resident otherwise known as a bum begging for money.  We decided that this was not the place to stay for the night so we drove some more and found a rest stop about an hour away. 

 

We had a crummy nights sleep as was to be expected in a roadside rest stop and on top of it all we were awoken the next morning at 5:30 AM local time with Giustina’s phone ringing off the hook.  We tried to answer it but since we were sound asleep at the time we didn’t get to the phone in time.  After we regained consciousness we checked the message and realized that on top of it it was a wrong number.  Since this was the third time we had gotten wrong number phone calls Giustina decided to return the call.  It turns out that the person calling a dean at a local vo tech school was looking for a professor.  He kept saying I was dialing 650465533, now we aren’t any rocket scientists but I think we can recognize a phone number when you see it.  The dean didn’t realize that the number he was trying to call, 3 times in the last few days wasn’t even a valid phone number!  And people wonder why most kids in America can’t write or do simple math.  We had a few more hours of driving to do and on the way we stopped by Wall, SD and the Wall Drug Store.  This drug store was advertised on roadside billboards from more than 600 miles away so we felt obligated to at least check it out and see.  As you might expect the town of Wall is not exactly a booming metropolis, in fact the Wall Drug Store takes up more than a block of the downtown area.  It was an amalgamation of junk store, clothing, diner, tourist mementos, and even gun shop.  They had everything EXCEPT a drug store.  Apparently the pharmacists who started the drug store had passed away quite a few years ago and their children just kept the store going.

 

We stayed in the town of Custer, SD at a decent campground but a pretty useless town.  Cruddy supermarket, no real restaurants.  The one night we wanted to eat at 8PM we ended up going to Pizza Hut because it was the ONLY place open.

 

We visited Mount Rushmore and Custer State Park, we saw a variety of animals and some really cool scenery.  Mount Rushmore itself was really impressive, it’s amazing that it was just one mans vision and insight that made it a reality.  We also visited the world’s third largest cave, Jewel Cave, nothing really special other than the fact that it was pretty big and dark.

 

We also had quite an introduction to how honorable people in this part of the country are.  First the office of the campground we stayed at was closed when we arrived with a note on the door saying pick any spot and then pay the next day.  Second the state park that we went to just had a sign with rates and a box to deposit your entrance fee.  And then the one that just put it all over the top was the driving range.  The range is open but there was no one there, you just go up pick up a bucket of balls and leave your money in a mailbox.  That’s it, I can’t even think of how that would work in downtown Boston.

 

We then moved from Custer, SD to Spearfish, SD, a little more north in the state.  We stayed in a nice local mom and pop campground with some very nice hosts.  They recommended a restaurant called the Bay Leaf Café which we ate at.  It was a pretty decent place and they had excellent seafood, something that we were dieing for after several weeks of beef eating.  We visited the nearby town of Deadwood and saw Wild Bill’s death chair at the Saloon #10.  All up and down the main street of Deadwood were mom and pop casino’s, it was pretty odd to see a casino / 7 11 / hotel.  We also visited Lead, SD and the largest gold mine in the US called the Homestake gold mine.  That evening on our way to dinner Giustina was driving down main street in Spearfish when a police car appeared in the rear view mirror.  It was the nicest police officer I have ever heard, he was so nice.  We didn’t think we did anything wrong but the officer said we were going 36 in a 25 mph zone, can you believe that?  11 mph over the speed limit in a 25 zone?  He obviously had nothing better to do except bother us but since we were just visiting he let us off with just a warning, how nice…

 

Next we left Spearfish, SD on our way through Wyoming.  We visited Devils Tower National Monument.  It was a pretty ominous site to see, it just kinda hangs out there in the middle of no where, pretty neat.  We also saw a variety of cows, horses, prairie dogs, sheep, and deer a long the way.

October 15, 2000

Onward we traveled to Milwaukee, WI.  We had to go here mostly because we saw a CBS Sunday Morning TV show about a butterfly exhibit at the local museum.  We pulled into a campground that was located a few miles south of the city called Jellystone campground, just like Yogi bear.  Apparently Hanna Barbara created a sort of campground franchise and they have these parks all over the place.  The first day in Milwaukee we decided to just bum around the city and see what it was like.  It was a Sunday morning and the town was as desolate as Fenway Park during the world series.  We were walking around trying to see if anything was open, just about everything we say was closed, until we say a line of parents and their children shuffling into a large building.  We approached the entrance and saw that they were all going to a Disney on ice show.  Well it seemed unlikely that we would be able to go but Giustina really wanted to see the show.  We went inside and much to my surprise we got in without a problem.  The one thing Disney does really well is sell and market itself.  They had Disney cups, Disney cotton candy, Disney popcorn, Disney lights and glow wands, and everything was of course at Disney prices.  The show itself was one of the better ice skating shows we have seen.  I didn’t even fall asleep once.

 

The next day we went to the Butterfly Exhibit at the local museum.  For those of you who didn’t see that CBS Sunday Morning show I’ll do my best to describe it.  You enter the butterfly wing through a kind of air lock by passing in through the first door, closing that door, and then entering through a second door.  This keeps all of the butterflies from flying away into the other areas of the museum.  Through the second door you enter the butterfly area which is a fairly small room about 30 feet by 15 feet.  The room is decorated with all different types of tropical plants and rocks with a main path along the middle length of the room.  All around fly hundreds of different species of butterflies, they are on the plants, the rocks, the walls, and sometimes as Giustina found out, if you stand still enough they even land on you.  It was a very cool experience and we highly recommend it to anyone.  The rest of the museum was pretty boring but the butterfly wing more than made up for it.

 

The next stop on our journey was the Miller Brewing Company.  Miller has a major presence in Milwaukee and their brewing factory takes up several city blocks.  The tour began by allowing us to walk through their processing facility behind some Plexiglas walls as we watched case after case of Miller beer being packaged for shipping.  Oh and just in case any Kellogg executives read this the Miller tour was FREE.  The end of the tour was the highlight as we got to sample some free beer, well Miller anyway but it was free.  After this it was on to the gift shop as all tours seem to end up in the gift shop.  We were actually able to purchase some new Titlest golf balls for less than $2 / ball, pretty cool, thanks Miller.  That evening we ate at an excellent German restaurant called Karl Ratzsch’s.  It was recommended by both Zagat and the book Eat Your Way Across the USA and it did not disappoint us.  Not only did it have excellent German beer on tap but the food was much better then I ever expected. 

 

On our final day in the Milwaukee area we decided to take in some golfing at the Blackwolf Run golf course.  This was an absolutely spectacular facility.  Everyone was very helpful and nice and the two courses and supporting facilities were some of the best we have ever played.  We played the Meadow Valley course and it was in excellent shape.  However it was also the most difficult course either of us have ever played.  There was so much sand always in the place where your ball wanted to land that I felt like a beach comber.  We couldn’t get the ball to stay on the green from 50 yards out with a sand wedge.  The greens were rolling and curving always away from the hole location.  By the time we got on the one hold that had an almost flat green neither of us remembered how to putt on a flat green.  It really kicked our butts and I would welcome the challenge of playing it again after several more years of practice.

 

Our next venture in Wisconsin brought us to the town of Wisconsin Dells and Lake Delton.  These towns probably have the highest concentration of tourist trap businesses we have ever seen.  Mile after mile of cheesy little museums like the Circus World Museum, tee shirt stores, useless trinkets, miniature golf by the trunk load, small mom and pop amusement parks with the dumbest themes like Noah's Ark - America's Largest Waterpark etc.  Fortunately for us most of these places were closed for the season but the one place we were there for was open, Tommy Bartlett’s Robot World.  By the time we arrived in town it was too late to go to Robot World so instead we opted for the standard tourist trap museum Ripley’s Believe it or Not.  It had the standard concoction of the most bizarre items you can imagine including a car made completely out of wood and a bowl made out of a human skull.  After we got our fill of the bizarre we went next door to the local Wax Museum where they had some of the worst recreations of American movie stars, celebrities, and other famous personalities.  The only thing that reminded us that these wax figures were actually who they said they were were the little name plates beneath each figure.  The next day we wanted to get a taste of some real Wisconsin cheese.  You are always told that the best cheeses in America come from Wisconsin, but so far on our journey we saw no trace of any type of cheese making operation.  Finally in this little tourist trap mecca we found a brochure for a local cheese “factory”.  We had been somewhat skeptical of these types of factories given our previous pretzel factory experiences, but I have to say we were greatly surprised.  When we approached the Carr Valley Cheese Factory I thought we were in for another pretzel experience.  The place was a small free standing building in the middle of no where.  There was one large tractor trailer on the side and a couple cars in the small parking lot.  Upon entry to the building the woman behind the counter immediately deduced that we were there for the free tour that was advertised on the brochure.  She brought us to a wall which displayed pictures of the cheese making process and began a short audio tape that described what was happening in each of the pictures.  Although a bit amateurish it was very informative.  After the audio presentation we were able to go through the factory, really just two main rooms, and see cheese in the process of being made.  We were able to walk right up to the workers making the cheese, talk to them, ask them questions, and pretty much do what ever we wanted.  It was a very refreshing change from the very structured tours that most large corporations put on like our previous Miller tour.  We got to talk with the employees and learn a little about their lives as well as the cheese making process.  All in all it was a very informative tour and the major corporations of the world would be well to copy what the Carr Valley Cheese Factory folks have done.

 

After we appropriately gorged ourselves on cheese we were on to the main event, Tommy Bartlett’s Robot World.  Now why this attraction had to have Tommy Bartlett’s name strewn across it I’ll never know, perhaps he was just egotistical and wanted to be remembered, and why it’s called Robot World I will also never know since we didn’t really see any robots.  Robot World is laid out like a walk through attraction you start by entering a space ship, actually it is an elevator decorated like a space ship, that transports you into Robot World.  Then you see your first and last look at a robot who introduces Robot World to you.  After this you walk around going through a variety of high school science fair like exhibits like see how light looks as it goes through this prism or figure out how to stack these 20 nails on top of each other so they do not touch the table.  For the most part the exhibits are fairly lame, there were two good exhibits though.  The first was a virtual reality game where you could play goalie in a soccer match.  The second was a Van de Graf generator that you could walk up to and touch.  You would never see this type of exhibit at Disney World though.  They actually let you walk right up to it and if you leaned over far enough, as I found mistakenly, you get a really large shock.  It snapped so loud it sounded like a gun went off.  Other than that it was a little bit of a let down experience.

 

Having been let down by man’s scientific creativity, we decided to drive on to La Crosse, WI and the mighty Mississippi river.  We selected a campground that was right on the Mississippi and got a site where our bedroom window looked right over the river.  It was really awesome to see.  Even though the river isn’t that wide up north here, it was still an amazing site to see.  We went on a cruise on the river boat the La Crosse Queen.  It was a nice boat and a fairly decent tour including historical accounts of how some of the original settlers had to build various dam creations to help raise the water level of the river in order to allow larger boats to ship cargo.

October 8, 2000

Our next stop on our trip was Ohio, we decided to stay in Oberlin Ohio just south west of Cleveland.  This allowed us to be near Cleveland, Akron, Canton, and Sandusky Ohio.  The first day we decided to trek down to Canton Ohio and the Pro Football Hall of Fame.  Giustina really enjoyed this as she got to see Joe Montana, Jerry Rice, and Steve Young’s memorabilia.  After this we drove up to Akron Ohio to visit Inventure Place, the inventor’s museum.  They had some interesting displays on inventions and random scientific stuff, and best of all we got there an hour before they closed so we didn’t have to pay.  It really wasn’t worth the $7.50 to get in just to see a bunch of patent numbers on the walls.  We were a little hungry after all this scientific learning so we decided to go to a place recommended by a friend of mine called the Skyway Drive-in.  Now with a name like that I wasn’t really sure what to expect.  Perhaps a drive up teller window with food, no.  Maybe a movie with dinner like the old fashioned drive in’s, nope.  It was a 50’s style diner where the waiter’s and waitresses come out and serve you at your car.  Course we didn’t know this going in and had to make asses out of ourselves as we tried to walk into the building where they cooked the food.  Once we figured out what was going on with the ordering process we actually had a pretty good greasy spoon burger. 

 

The next day we decided to spend some quality time on the golf course.  We golfed at a course called Fowler’s Mill.  It was supposedly the best course in Ohio and since we didn’t have any other Ohio courses to compare it too it may very well have been.  In comparing it to the other great courses that we have played I would rank it in the middle of the pack.  Nothing really great stood out about the course.  After golf we took a drive along lake Erie toward a local amusement park called Cedar Point.  The drive was very nice, this was the first chance we had to see one of the Great Lakes and we were not disappointed, it was really huge.  And speaking of huge, the houses along the lake were enormous.  Some of them would of cost multiple millions of dollars back in Boston.  The Cedar Point amusement park is known for having some of the best, biggest, and highest roller coasters in the world and they definitely did not disappoint us.  Because we were there during the evening not all the coasters were open but we did get to go on three of them.  The first one called The Raptor was one of the new designs in roller coasters made of metal pipe tubing and shoulder harness seats.  Now it’s been quite a number of months since we had been on a roller coaster and we probably shouldn’t have started with this one.  It shook us up, down, upside down, around and every which way.  When we got off it took me 5 minutes just to figure out that my stomach didn’t really belong in my throat.  After we walked around for a while, I felt an urging desire to subject my body to another one.  The next coaster we went on is called The Millennium Force and is the highest coaster in the world.  As we waited in line I caught a glimpse of the first hill, it was so high I could barely see the top of it.  The people on top look so small they looked like gnats.  We got into our seats and the bar lowered across our laps.  I thought to myself this is it, just this little bar?  The last coaster we were on practically prevented any part of your body from moving, all this one did was put a one inch bar between me and sudden death.  As the coaster started out it went up the highest hill faster then any other coaster I had ever been on.  Once up top I felt like I could touch the stars, I was happy that there were lights on the coaster so that low flying planes could avoid it.  As we crossed the top and started our fall back to earth I felt like I was free falling.  It was an incredible feeling of adrenaline.  Our last coaster was The Mantis and it was unlike any other we had been on.  First there was no seat, you stand and are strapped in.  Then the coaster goes through five loops inside out upside down right side left etc.  By the time we got off I had no idea which side was up down or in between.  It was a hell of a ride.

 

The next stop on our journey was going to be Chicago, but that was about 5 and a half hours away and we didn’t really feel like driving that far.  So instead we looked at the map and searched for something in between.  Well there isn’t a ton of places to go between Oberlin Ohio and Chicago Illinois so we picked Battle Creek Michigan.  Why not go and see where Frosted Flakes are made.  Even though Battle Creek isn’t a really big town, there are no real campgrounds in downtown Battle Creek, so we decided to stay in Union City Michigan just south of Battle Creek.  Our sole purpose for coming here was too see the Kellogg Plant.  Well we quickly learned that Kellogg doesn’t give plant tours anymore but that we could spend a visit to Kellogg’s Cereal City.  That sounded good enough.  We tried to go on Monday but they were closed so we went to the Underground railroad monument and golfed instead.  The next day we went back to the tour to see.  On arrival we realized that we had to pay to go in.  Can you believe that we paid money to be marketed to and sold cereal subconsciously.  What kind of marketing genius thought of this one.  And it wouldn’t be that bad if it was a few dollars but they wanted $6.50 a person.  Well we had to pay, I didn’t drive to Battle Creek for nothing.  We paid our $13 dollars and went inside.  Inside we were overcome with a deluge of Kellogg memorabilia and advertisements.  They had a mock assembly line displaying how cereal is made.  A series of televisions showing old Kellogg commercials.  And  a photo booth where you could have your face plastered on a box of Kellogg’s Corn Flakes.  So of course in keeping with this obvious display of commercialism and blatant marketing we had to pay an additional $16.50 to have our faces put on a box.  Oh well long live capitalism.

 

Our next stop was Chicago, the windy city.  There weren’t many RV parks in the greater Chicago area but we picked the one that was closest to downtown since we figured we would be driving in and out of town quite a bit.  It was called the Windy City RV park, I only tell you this because no one should ever have to stay at this dump again.  It began when we tried to get a space for our RV, now they told us the had one that would fit a 35 foot 102 inch wide motor home and it did JUST fit.  We had enough room for the RV, the door to swing open and a picnic table.  You could barley walk around the RV with the amount of space that we had.  If the next door neighbor had their window open I could have spit on their dining room table from inside our RV.  Ok so it was a little tight no big deal, then came our first night in the park.  Well, as I said before the park was about as close to Chicago as you could get without actually being in Chicago.  Because of this we were strategically positioned RIGHT NEXT to the highway which was very convenient.  On the other hand the noise from the highway lasted night and day.  You could constantly hear the roar of the 18 wheelers engines and the cruised by the park at 70 miles an hour.  It was like living on a runway with planes constantly flying over you.  Ok so two strikes not so bad.  Well the final straw came the next morning as we arose from our crappy nights sleep still with the thoughts of roaring trucks in our head.  As I went to take a shower it smelled a little funny.  Now in an RV every once in a while you get a strange odor from the holding tanks if things aren’t as clean as they could be and that’s all I thought it was.  Until I actually got into the shower and realized that the stench was coming from the water itself.  It stunk, just like a sulfur pit combined with spoiled eggs and rotten garbage all mixed into one.    Ah well I guess that’s what you get when you are just looking for location.

 

We spent the next day site seeing in downtown Chicago.  We saw the Aquarium, Planetarium, and the Navy Pier.  After this we felt a little hungry and what better way to quench that hunger than with an original slice of Chicago’s deep dish pizza.  And as per the recommendation of Mr. Stormy we decided we would go to Gino’s East.  Unfortunatly we were on the other side of town and didn’t feel like driving into downtown so we decided to take a cab instead.  Now having lived in Boston for a number of years we were all used to cab drivers and the dangers that they bring.  The erratic driving, running up the fare by going the wrong direction, smelly cabs etc.  Well I have to say if our cab driver is any indication of the cab drivers in Chicago every cabbie should be required by law to go to Chicago to learn.  The ride started out rather poorly as the cab driver misheard the destination that we were going to and by the time he realized it we were way out of wack.  So following standard cabby protocol he turned the meter off (it read about $4.80) and proceeded to our real destination.  On our way I figured that when we got there I would be spending a good deal of time arguing with him as to how much to pay.  I figured it would probably cost about $10.  Well we got to the address where we thought Gino’s was only to find out that Gino’s moved.  So the cab driver asked a local bell hop if he knew where it moved to and got the new address.  He then proceeded on to the new location still with the meter turned off.  Now at this point I’m thinking this is going to cost about $20 if we are lucky.  Well we arrived at Gino’s and as we were exiting the cab the cabby said $4.80.  Wow can you believe that no additional charge, no raising the price nothing.  We felt so bad for the guy that he had to drive us all over town that I gave him $10 and he was unusually thankful.  We had excellent pizza at Gino’s and we highly recommend this place to anyone who wants original Chicago deep dish pizza.

 

The next day we decided to spend some quality time on the links.  We played a round at the site of the PGA tour’s Western Open, Cog Hill.  The course was very nice and a bit challenging at times but not overwhelming.  That evening we went to Morton’s of Chicago.  Now anyone who knows us knows that we love red meat and Morton’s did not disappoint.  I had a beautifully cooked porterhouse and Giustina had a melt in your mouth Filet.  It was absolutely delicious.

 

On our last day in town we did some more of the touristy type things.  We figured we would go to the world famous Museum of Science and Industry.  Now the museum itself was very nice, however getting into the museum was quite a chore.  It started with a drive into the parking lot from hell.  This lot was probably as big as Foxboro stadium and the spaces were as small as the Rowes Wharf garage.  Finally after level and level of filled parking spaces we found an empty space.  Then we going walking into the entryway of the museum to purchase tickets.  Now at most museums in the world you walk process.  Fortunatly there was a special line for this type of procedure and as we waited I looked over to see where the regular folks were purchasing their tickets.  WOW, their line looked like the because of our patience during this delay all general admission tickets would be FREE.  Can you believe that FREE wow what a bargin.  Then I remembered that we already purchased our tickets in advance and that they were non-refundable, DOH.  The museum itself was very nice, I wouldn’t exactly say it was world class but it was fun.

September 30, 2000

It is Sunday September 24th and our journey has officially begun.  We have left the confines of our parent’s driveway for the cruel realities of the United States.  Our first destination the city of brotherly love about 2 and ½ hours south.  Well not really the city, a northern suburb of the city, which was fine with me since I really didn’t want to drive a 35 foot RV into downtown Philadelphia anyway.  The purpose of our first stop is to visit with some long lost friends of ours, Dean and Nicole.  We were anxious to see Dean and Nicole because we hadn’t seen them in over a year and they had a new baby daughter Sophie that we hadn’t seen since she was first born.  The trip from northeast Pennsylvania to the northern suburbs of Philly was pretty uneventful, just a straight shot down the northeast extension of the PA turnpike.  We didn’t want to make it very complicated seeing as how this was our first real trip.  We arrived at Dean and Nicole’s home late Sunday afternoon.  We had a great time catching up with them and playing with Sophie.  Sophie had just turned one year old about 2 months ago and she is an adorable baby.  She played with all of her toys and we watched a Sesame Street video.  I had no idea how many of the old Sesame Street songs I could actually remember from Ernie’s rubber ducky to the Count’s counting song to Oscar’s I Love Trash.  I have to tell you that I have no idea how parents ever took care of their kids before the invention on the VCR.  It is amazing how mesmerizing a Sesame Street video can be to a one year old. (Or for that matter a 29 year old)  We stayed the night with our friends and the next morning headed off to our next destination Amish country and Lancaster Pennsylvania.

 

On the next leg of our trip we decided to get a little more adventurous in our route selection.  I have always thought that if you want to see America you should “see America”, so we decided to take our RV off the Interstate on onto some of the more scenic roads.  We strolled along the winding 2 lane roads on our way to our campsite in Intercourse, Pennsylvania.  Can you believe someone actually named a city Intercourse?  What were they thinking?  Anyway, the drive again was fairly straight forward, the only difficulty was trying to keep the 102in wide RV between the double yellow lines and the edge of the road.  It seems since most of the Amish don’t have cars, they ride in horse and buggies, that the roads they built around the area weren’t really built to accommodate our width.  It proved a little challenging but I managed to get us to our campsite at Beacon campground.  The campground itself was fairly nice much more subdued than our first campground experience in Massachusetts (see below).  The next morning it was raining so we decided to spend it indoors at the Pennsylvania Railroad Museum.  We saw many different types of locomotives and railroad cars including several large electric locomotives.  After the museum we went across the street to take a ride on the Strasburg railroad.  The railroad was a restored passenger train with a steam locomotive.  Even though it was raining the passenger cars were enclosed so we were dry and it was a nice ride through the Amish countryside.  After the train ride we decided to keep with the train motif for the day and visit the National Toy Train Museum.  They had all different types of toy trains including ones from the 1800’s that were “child powered”, meaning you just pushed them, to some beautiful brass locomotives from Japan. 

 

After the Toy Train Museum it continued to rain outside and seeing how most of the shops in the area were outside we were at a little bit of a loss as to what to do.  Some where along the way we had picked up a tourist pamphlet for the Intercourse Pretzel Factory with free factory tours.  This seemed like an interesting indoor activity so we decided to try and find this factory.  We had the name of the place where the factory was but didn’t see any factory looking building.  Finally we spotted a sign in a strip mall for the “pretzel factory”.  We went inside and sure enough this small little place in a room that couldn’t have been more than 20 by 20 was the factory.  We went up to the counter to ask the young Amish girl if they gave free tours as it stated on their pamphlet.  She said they did and the next one would be starting in about 20 minutes.  We decided since we had nothing better to do and it was still raining out that we would wait and go on the “tour”.  As we waited we purchased a soft cinnamon pretzel and ate it as we strolled around the shop looking at the items that they had on display.  You would expect that a pretzel factory would have pretzel type memorabilia, well since this one wasn’t much of a factory, there was no reason for them to sell pretzel type memorabilia.  Instead they had tons of junk, and I use the term junk because no other term would describe the amount of useless stuff that this place had.  Everything from a 1920 mixing bowl to a cook book from 1973 about how to make things made with cinnamon, to a pair of leather baby shoes, to an old fashioned wooden coat rack.  And everything had a price on it, I think you probably could have bought the Amish girl behind the counter for the right price.  As we strolled through the wads of stuff waiting for our tour to begin a bus load of senior citizens came into the store.  They were part of some sort of group like the Retired Pretzel Union people or some such nonsense.  This group of senior citizens seemed to make as much noise as a bus load of high school teenagers just let out for spring break.  Also keep in mind that the room is still 20 x 20 with all of that useless junk that now the bus load of old people seemed so fascinated with.  Then a woman came out from a room in back and said that the tour was about to begin.  She led us into a back room that was only slightly larger than the front one.  It was divided by a waist high counter that corralled all of the visitors into a 3 x 10 foot area with the remaining space left over for the “pretzel factory”.  The factory consisted of about half a dozen pieces of machinery each designed to perform some particular pretzel making step.  The woman clamored on for about 10 minutes about the history and other useless facts about pretzels that I can’t seem to remember.  After this our tour was complete, and I thanked god as we left that it had stopped raining so we didn’t have to go to the local Intercourse Potato Chip Factory.

 

This about completed our trip to Lancaster, we did spend an enjoyable evening at the local American Music Theater.  They had a show that was showcasing the songs of the 40s and 50s.  After this it was on to our next stop Beaver Pennsylvania and a visit to our friends Nancy, Jim and family.  We arrived in Beaver early in the evening and spent the early evening playing with Nancy and Jim’s kids Mary Katherine, Annie, and Michael Joseph.  That evening we had a good diner at a local restaurant with Nancy and Jim and got caught up on the going’s on in all of our lives.  It also gave Nancy and Jim an excuse to have Grandma come over and baby sit their kids for an evening, something which I think they were looking forward too.  The next day we spent the time around the Beaver area.  We went to a local playground and had fun playing on the swings, the slide, and the various other children rides that I had long forgotten from my youth.  We spent the afternoon in downtown Beaver which was a really nice town.  One of the only real downtowns with actual stores and shops that you could walk between.  No strip malls, no Wal-Mart Super Center, no McDonalds.  Just your local mom and pop stores with real people in a real neighborhood.  It was nice to see that hometown America is still alive and well in Beaver.

October 1, 2000

Ok so we haven’t updated the site in a while, but we have been pretty busy (Well maybe not that busy, just lazy).  We visited Steamtown, a local National Park and train museum.  We took a train ride through Lackawanna county and saw a lot of old time steam engines.  Then the first week in September we closed on our condo in Boston.  So we are now officially homeless out of work bums. 

 

After this you would think that we would have left PA to start our journey but that was not the case.  Because the state of MA in it’s infinite wisdom decided to have our RV registration expire in October right in the middle of our trip we needed to transfer it to PA.  You would think that this should be a fairly quick process but nooooo.  We found out that we couldn’t just register the RV in PA, the state needed to transfer the title from MA to a PA title.  Well that would be easy except for the fact that we didn’t have the title for the RV, our bank did.  So we have been waiting now for about a week to get the title from our bank to no avail.

 

So in the meantime we have spent some additional time golfing and visiting our relatives.  We also got a chance to go to Niagara falls.  We were only there a couple hours but it was a pretty awesome site to see.

 

So the latest plan is to leave this Sunday, the 24th of September, we will see…

August 13, 2000

Well the time finally came for our first real  RV trip.  The trip was a journey from Pennsylvania to Boston.  We planned to spend the first couple nights in our condo in Cambridge while we left the RV in a campground for safe keeping.  We had a little difficulty trying to find a spot in a campground, it seemed that a lot of parents decided that August is a good time to take a final family summer vacation.  After calling every campground in the greater Boston area we finally found one “spot” in Mansfield, MA.  It seemed a little too good to be true, but who were we to look a gift horse in the mouth.

 

Finally after an exhausting 6 hour drive from PA to Mansfield we arrived at the exit off of 495 around 8:30PM.  In following the directions to the campground we discovered that the city of Mansfield decided to tear up the road from 495 to the campground.  How nice of them considering it was difficult enough to keep a 35 foot RV on a 2 lane road, why not make it more difficult for us by removing the pavement and adding in a few dozen pot holes.  Well we finally get through the road from hell and arrived at the campground.  We park just outside the “office” (The only reason I call it an office is that it is the only building that does not have wheels) and I go in to check in and get our space.  As I am talking with the woman behind the counter I can’t help but notice that of the 20 or so people who are milling around the office, only myself and the woman behind the counter are the ones who are wearing a shirt with sleeves.  I think to myself, Ok it’s hot out I can kinda understand that…and I moved on.  During the process of checking in the woman behind the counter tells me that we will have space 11G tonight and then tomorrow we will have to move to space 12G.  Ok, no big deal since we were only using it for a parking space tonight anyway.  She gives me a map to our space and points me in the correct direction.

 

I go back out to the RV and drive through their “security gate” which consists of a piece of PVC piping connected to an arm that goes up and down, I feel safer already.  We start to drive through the campground and notice that EVERYONE in the campground has Christmas lights hung up.  Some have them on their campers, some on trees, but they are everywhere, it really looks like Disney World with all the lights.  The other thing we realize at this point is that the roads in the campground really just weren’t build for a 102 inch wide 35 foot motor home.  As we navigate at 5 mph through the park we finally arrive at the section where our space was located.  A really nice fellow camper points our space out to us, since by now it was completely dark and there are no lights, other than those of the Christmas variety around.  We look to the space next to the one where we are supposed to go into and can’t help but take notice of what I would call a 37 foot Christmas tree with windows and a door.  It was a Fleetwood Bounder motor home with so many Christmas lights on it, it took me a few minutes to figure out that there was a motor home underneath.  There were lights on the large awning, lights on the slide out awning, lights strung from tree to tree, lights on all sides, and in the front a bunch of colored icicle lights hanging down over the windshield.  After we got over our amazement and regained our sight from the light blindness we backed our motor home into the site and left it for the night as we ventured into Cambridge.

 

The next day we had to drive back to Mansfield because if you remember we had to move our motor home from space 11G to the space next door 12G.  After we arrived at the campground and passed through the “security” gate again we drove through the campground and noticed that a large number of the inhabitants really don’t understand the concept of MOBILE in mobile home.  Some folks have brought their full size porch swings, their screened in porches usually bigger than their actual trailers, more flowers and garden crap than you would find at Home Depot, and satellite dishes as you would expect we noticed one satellite dish that was permanently mounted to a nearby tree.  As we arrived at our space, comforted in the fact that it was still there, we realized that space 12G was occupied by that Fleetwood Bounder with the 10,000 Christmas lights strung around it.  This presented a problem because there was no one around it packing anything up or moving the RV in anyway.  They even had the water and power lines for the RV buried in an underground trench so they wouldn’t have to step over them.  Since the campground only gave us the time between check out at 1:30PM and check in at 2PM to move our RV we were perplexed as to what to do.  We drove back up to the campground “office” and discussed our situation with the owner’s wife.  She said that the folks in space 12G probably forgot that they were supposed to leave today.  Apparently they had been around for a while and also owed the campground a good deal of money.  The woman said she would have her husband talk to folks in 12G to get them to move but that in the meantime we would need to move our RV to a different space.  I reluctantly obliged her since I didn’t have much choice, keeping in mind that all the other campgrounds were sold out. 

 

As we left the office we ran into the owner.  He was a rather large man about 6 feet tall and around 300 pounds.  It was also a rather humid day, so I found it odd that he was wearing a blue sweatshirt and maroon sweatpants.  He stated that he talked to the folks in 12G and they misunderstood when their last day was, but that they would be checked out in a few hours.  Well we still had a great deal of packing to do back in Cambridge so we really didn’t have a couple of hours to waste hanging around the RV park.  In the process of talking to him he was having a second conversation with another “gentleman” friend of his.  They were discussing the fact that another patron had abandoned a trailer on their campground with several cats and he had to go inside this trailer for some random reason.  As he was talking to us in mid conversation he picked something off of his chest.  Then he jumped around like he was trying to shake something off of his body in what seemed like an epileptic fit.  Apparently the cats that were left in the abandoned trailer had flees.  Then near the end of his conversation with us, he said “Excuse me a minute” and proceeded to rip off his sweatshirt thereby exposing his somewhat round physique and threw his shirt to the ground trying to beat the flees out of it as he jumped around.  At this point in the conversation we decided to excuse ourselves and just come back tomorrow.
